Unit 8: Thermodynamics

Basic concept

Thermodynamics is a field of physics that deals with the relationships between heat and other forms of energy. It studies how energy is transferred in the form of heat, work, and matter, and how it can be converted from one form to another. The principles of thermodynamics are expressed in four laws. The zeroth law defines temperature and thermal equilibrium. The first law, also known as the law of energy conservation, states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or changed from one form to another. The second law introduces the concept of entropy, stating that systems will naturally progress towards a state of disorder. The third law states that absolute zero cannot be achieved. These laws are fundamental to our understanding of the physical world, from microscopic particles to massive stars, and even the universe itself.

Thermal equilibrium,

Question: Two bodies A and B of different masses are kept in contact in an isolated system. If no heat is allowed to flow into or out of the system and the bodies are allowed to come to thermal equilibrium, which of the following statements is true?

Options:

1.       The final temperature of body A will be higher than that of body B.

2.       The final temperature of body B will be higher than that of body A.

3.       The final temperatures of both bodies will be the same.

4.       The bodies will not come to thermal equilibrium.

Answer: Option 3. The final temperatures of both bodies will be the same.

Explanation: Thermal equilibrium is a state in which all parts of a system are at the same temperature. So, when two bodies come into contact and reach thermal equilibrium, they will have the same final temperature, regardless of their initial temperatures or masses. This is in accordance with the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics.

 

Question: Two bodies, A and B, are in thermal contact with each other. Body A has a mass of 2 kg and a specific heat capacity of 420 J/kg.K. It is initially at a temperature of 100°C. Body B has a mass of 3 kg and a specific heat capacity of 390 J/kg.K. It is initially at a temperature of 20°C. If no heat is allowed to flow into or out of the system, what will be the final temperature when the two bodies reach thermal equilibrium?

Options:

1.     60°C

2.     70°C

3.     80°C

4.     90°C

Answer: Option 2. 70°C

Explanation: When two bodies come into contact and reach thermal equilibrium, they will have the same final temperature. The heat lost by body A will be equal to the heat gained by body B. This can be expressed as:

m₁c₁(T₁ - T) = m₂c₂(T - T₂)

where m is the mass, c is the specific heat capacity, T is the final temperature, and the subscripts 1 and 2 refer to body A and body B respectively.

Substituting the given values into this equation gives:

2 kg * 420 J/kg.K * (100°C - T) = 3 kg * 390 J/kg.K * (T - 20°C)

Solving this equation for T gives a final temperature of approximately 70°C.

Heat, work and internal energy.

Question: Which of the following statements correctly distinguishes between the concepts of heat and temperature?

Options:

1.       Heat is a measure of the total energy in a system, while temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a system.

2.       Heat is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a system, while temperature is a measure of the total energy in a system.

3.       Heat and temperature both refer to the same concept - the total energy in a system.

Answer: Option 1. Heat is a measure of the total energy in a system, while temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a system.

Explanation: Heat and temperature are related but distinct concepts. Heat refers to the total energy contained in a system due to the motion (kinetic energy) and arrangement (potential energy) of particles. It is measured in joules. On the other hand, temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a system. It gives an indication of how hot or cold an object is and is measured in degrees Celsius (°C), Fahrenheit (°F), or Kelvin (K). It’s important to note that while heat transfer results in a change in temperature, they are not the same thing.

Question: A 2 kg aluminum block initially at 20°C is heated by a 100 W source for 10 minutes. Given that the specific heat capacity of aluminum is 0.897 J/g°C, what will be the final temperature of the aluminum block assuming no heat is lost to the surroundings?

Options:

1.      40°C

2.      60°C

3.      80°C

4.      100°C

Answer: Option 2. 60°C

Explanation: The heat absorbed by the aluminum block can be calculated using the formula Q = mcΔT, where Q is the heat absorbed, m is the mass, c is the specific heat capacity, and ΔT is the change in temperature.

The heat provided by the source is Pt, where P is the power of the source and t is the time. Given that P = 100 W and t = 10 minutes = 600 seconds, we have Q = Pt = 100 W * 600 s = 60000 J.

Setting Q from the source equal to Q absorbed by the block gives us:

60000 J = 2 kg * 0.897 J/g°C * ΔT

Solving this equation for ΔT gives us a change in temperature of approximately 40°C. Therefore, the final temperature of the aluminum block will be its initial temperature plus this change, or 20°C + 40°C = 60°C.

 

 

Question: A gas expands in a cylinder against a constant external pressure of 1 atm. The initial volume of the gas is 10 L. If the gas does 821.4 J of work on the surroundings while expanding, what is the final volume of the gas? (Note: 1 L·atm = 101.3 J)

Options:

1.       15 L

2.       20 L

3.       25 L

4.       30 L

Answer: Option 3. 25 L

Explanation: In thermodynamics, the work done by a gas expanding against a constant external pressure is given by W = PΔV, where W is the work done, P is the pressure, and ΔV is the change in volume.

Given that W = 821.4 J and P = 1 atm, we can convert the work to L·atm by dividing by 101.3 J/L·atm to get W = 8.11 L·atm.

Substituting these values into the equation gives us:

8.11 L·atm = 1 atm * ΔV

Solving this equation for ΔV gives us a change in volume of approximately 8.11 L.

Therefore, the final volume of the gas will be its initial volume plus this change, or 10 L + 8.11 L = 18.11 L ≈ 18 L.

 

Question: A system absorbs 500 J of heat and does 200 J of work on the surroundings. If the initial internal energy of the system was 1000 J, what is the final internal energy of the system?

Options:

1.       1300 J

2.       1500 J

3.       1700 J

4.       1900 J

Answer: Option 1. 1300 J

Explanation: In thermodynamics, the change in internal energy (ΔU) of a system is given by the first law of thermodynamics: ΔU = Q - W, where Q is the heat absorbed by the system and W is the work done by the system on its surroundings.

Given that Q = 500 J and W = 200 J, we can substitute these values into the equation to get ΔU = 500 J - 200 J = 300 J.

Therefore, the final internal energy of the system will be its initial internal energy plus this change, or 1000 J + 300 J = 1300 J.

Question: A Carnot engine operates between two reservoirs at temperatures of 800 K and 400 K. If the engine absorbs 2400 J of heat from the hot reservoir in each cycle, what is the amount of work done by the engine in each cycle?

Options:

1.       800 J

2.       1200 J

3.       1600 J

4.       2000 J

Answer: Option 2. 1200 J

Explanation: The efficiency of a Carnot engine is given by 1 - (Tc/Th), where Tc is the temperature of the cold reservoir and Th is the temperature of the hot reservoir. Substituting the given values, we get an efficiency of 1 - (400 K / 800 K) = 0.5 or 50%.

The work done by the engine is equal to the efficiency times the heat absorbed from the hot reservoir. Therefore, the work done is 0.5 * 2400 J = 1200 J.

reversible and irreversible processes.

Question: Which of the following statements best describes a reversible process in thermodynamics?

2.       It is a process that can return to its initial state without leaving changes in the surroundings.

4.       It is a process that always decreases the entropy of the universe.

Answer: Option 2. It is a process that can return to its initial state without leaving changes in the surroundings.

Explanation: A reversible process in thermodynamics is an idealized process that can be reversed without leaving any trace on the surroundings. In other words, both the system and surroundings can be returned to their original states if the process is reversed. In reality, no process is completely reversible because some energy will always be lost to the surroundings as heat, making it impossible to return both the system and surroundings to their original states.

Carnot engine and its efficiency.

Question: Which of the following statements best describes a Carnot engine in thermodynamics?

Options:

1.       It is an engine that operates between two reservoirs at the same temperature.

2.       It is an engine that operates between two reservoirs at different temperatures and has the highest efficiency possible.

3.       It is an engine that operates between two reservoirs at different temperatures and has the lowest efficiency possible.

4.       It is an engine that operates between two reservoirs at different temperatures and its efficiency depends on the specific heat capacities of the reservoirs.

Answer: Option 2. It is an engine that operates between two reservoirs at different temperatures and has the highest efficiency possible.

Explanation: A Carnot engine is a theoretical heat engine that operates on the reversible Carnot cycle. The engine is an idealized model of heat engine which operates between two heat reservoirs at different temperatures. The work done in each cycle is maximized in a Carnot engine, making it the most efficient possible heat engine. This efficiency depends only on the temperatures of the hot and cold reservoirs, and not on the specific properties of the working substance or the specific nature of the work transfer.

 

Question: Which of the following statements best describes the efficiency of a Carnot engine in thermodynamics?

Options:

1.       The efficiency of a Carnot engine depends on the specific heat capacities of the hot and cold reservoirs.

3.       The efficiency of a Carnot engine is 1 minus the ratio of the temperature of the cold reservoir to the temperature of the hot reservoir.

4.       The efficiency of a Carnot engine is the ratio of the temperature of the cold reservoir to the temperature of the hot reservoir.

Answer: Option 3. The efficiency of a Carnot engine is 1 minus the ratio of the temperature of the cold reservoir to the temperature of the hot reservoir.

Explanation: The efficiency (η) of a Carnot engine, which is an idealized heat engine, is given by η = 1 - Tc/Th, where Tc is the absolute temperature (in Kelvin) of the cold reservoir and Th is the absolute temperature (in Kelvin) of the hot reservoir. This means that no real engine can be more efficient than a Carnot engine operating between the same temperatures.
