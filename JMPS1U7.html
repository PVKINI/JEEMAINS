Elastic behaviour,

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Elastic Behavior” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A spring of spring constant k is stretched by a force F causing an extension x. If the force applied is doubled, what will be the new extension in the spring?

Options:

1.     x

2.     2x

3.     4x

4.     x/2

Answer: The correct answer is Option 2: 2x.

Explanation: According to Hooke’s Law, the force F required to stretch or compress a spring by a distance x from its natural length is proportional to x, and is given by the formula F = kx, where k is the spring constant.

If the force applied is doubled, F becomes 2F. Substituting this into the formula, we get 2F = kx', where x' is the new extension.

Comparing this with the original formula, we find that x' = 2x.

Hence, the new extension in the spring is 2x.

 

Stress-strain relationship,

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Stress-Strain Relationship” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A wire of length L and cross-sectional area A is subjected to a force F that causes it to extend by ΔL. The material of the wire obeys Hooke’s Law. If the force applied is doubled, what will be the new extension in the wire?

Options:

1.     ΔL

2.     2ΔL

3.     4ΔL

4.     ΔL/2

Answer: The correct answer is Option 2: 2ΔL.

Explanation: According to Hooke’s Law, the stress σ in a material is directly proportional to the strain ε. Stress is defined as the force F divided by the cross-sectional area A, and strain is defined as the extension ΔL divided by the original length L. So, we have σ = F/A = kε = k(ΔL/L), where k is the proportionality constant.

If the force applied is doubled, F becomes 2F. Substituting this into the formula, we get σ' = 2F/A = kε' = k(ΔL'/L), where ΔL' is the new extension.

Comparing this with the original formula, we find that ΔL' = 2ΔL.

Hence, the new extension in the wire is 2ΔL.

Young’s modulus,

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Young’s Modulus” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A wire of length L and cross-sectional area A is made of a material with Young’s modulus Y. It is subjected to a tensile stress that causes an extension ΔL. If the length of the wire is doubled while keeping the applied force and the material of the wire constant, what will be the new extension in the wire?

Options:

1.     ΔL

2.     2ΔL

3.     ΔL/2

4.     4ΔL

Answer: The correct answer is Option 1: ΔL.

Explanation: According to the definition of Young’s modulus, Y = Stress/Strain = (F/A) / (ΔL/L), where F is the applied force.

If the length of the wire is doubled, L becomes 2L. However, since the applied force and the material of the wire are kept constant, the stress in the wire remains the same. Therefore, the strain in the wire (which is equal to Stress/Y) also remains the same.

Since strain is defined as the extension divided by the original length, and both the strain and original length have doubled, it follows that the extension ΔL' must remain constant.

Hence, the new extension in the wire is ΔL.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Bulk Modulus” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A solid cube of side a and volume V is subjected to a uniform pressure P that causes a decrease in its volume ΔV. The material of the cube has a bulk modulus B. If the pressure applied is doubled, what will be the new change in volume?

Options:

1.     ΔV

2.     2ΔV

3.     ΔV/2

Answer: The correct answer is Option 2: 2ΔV.

Explanation: According to the definition of bulk modulus, B = P/(ΔV/V), where P is the applied pressure.

If the pressure applied is doubled, P becomes 2P. Substituting this into the formula, we get B = 2P/(ΔV'/V), where ΔV' is the new change in volume.

Comparing this with the original formula, we find that ΔV' = 2ΔV.

Hence, the new change in volume is 2ΔV.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Modulus of Rigidity” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A solid cylinder of radius r and length L is subjected to a shearing force F that causes a lateral displacement x in its top surface. The material of the cylinder has a modulus of rigidity η. If the shearing force applied is doubled, what will be the new lateral displacement?

Options:

1.     x

2.     2x

3.     x/2

4.     4x

Answer: The correct answer is Option 2: 2x.

Explanation: According to the definition of modulus of rigidity, η = F/(A*x), where F is the applied shearing force, A is the area over which the force is applied (which is equal to πr² for a cylinder), and x is the lateral displacement.

If the shearing force applied is doubled, F becomes 2F. Substituting this into the formula, we get η = 2F/(A*x'), where x' is the new lateral displacement.

Comparing this with the original formula, we find that x' = 2x.

Hence, the new lateral displacement is 2x.

 

Pressure due to a fluid column;

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Pressure Due to Fluid Column” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A fluid column of height h and density ρ exerts a pressure P at its base. The acceleration due to gravity is g. If the height of the fluid column is doubled and its density is halved, what will be the new pressure at the base of the fluid column?

Options:

1.     P

2.     2P

3.     P/2

4.     4P

Answer: The correct answer is Option 1: P.

Explanation: The pressure P at the base of a fluid column is given by the formula P = ρgh, where ρ is the density of the fluid, g is the acceleration due to gravity, and h is the height of the fluid column.

If the height of the fluid column is doubled, h becomes 2h. If the density of the fluid is halved, ρ becomes ρ/2.

Substituting these values into the formula, we get P' = (ρ/2)g(2h) = ρgh = P.

Hence, the new pressure at the base of the fluid column is P.

 

Pascal’s law and its applications.

Q: A U-shaped tube contains water and mercury, as shown in the figure. The right arm of the tube has a cross-sectional area of 10 cm^2, while the left arm has a cross-sectional area of 5 cm^2. A force of 100 N is applied on the piston at the right end. What is the pressure difference between the water and mercury levels in the left arm?

A) 1.96 kPa B) 3.92 kPa C) 5.88 kPa D) 7.84 kPa

Answer: B) 3.92 kPa

Explanation: According to Pascal’s law, the pressure change at any point in a confined incompressible fluid is transmitted throughout the fluid such that the same change occurs everywhere. Therefore, the pressure exerted by the piston on the right end is equal to the pressure exerted by the water column on the mercury surface in the right arm.

Let P be the pressure at the mercury surface in both arms, h be the height difference between the water and mercury levels in the left arm, and g be the acceleration due to gravity. Then we have:

P + F/A = P + ρ_w g h

where F is the force applied by the piston, A is the cross-sectional area of the right arm, and ρ_w is the density of water.

Solving for h, we get:

h = F / (ρ_w g A)

Substituting the given values, we get:

h = 100 / (1000 x 9.8 x 0.001)

h = 1.02 m

The pressure difference between the water and mercury levels in the left arm is given by:

ΔP = ρ_w g h

Substituting the values, we get:

ΔP = 1000 x 9.8 x 1.02

ΔP = 3920 Pa

ΔP = 3.92 kPa

 

 

Viscosity.

Q: Which of the following statements is true about the viscosity of liquids?

A) It increases with increasing temperature B) It decreases with increasing temperature C) It does not depend on temperature D) It depends on the shape of the container

Answer: B) It decreases with increasing temperature

Explanation: The viscosity of liquids is a measure of their resistance to flow or deformation. It depends on the intermolecular forces between the liquid molecules, which are stronger when the molecules are closer together. When the temperature of a liquid increases, the molecules gain more kinetic energy and move faster, which reduces the intermolecular forces and makes the liquid less viscous. Therefore, the viscosity of liquids decreases with increasing temperature. For example, honey is more viscous than water at room temperature, but if both liquids are heated, honey becomes less viscous and flows more easily than water.

 

Stokes’ law. & Terminal velocity

Q: A small spherical ball of radius 1 mm and density 800 kg/m^3 falls through a viscous liquid of density 1000 kg/m^3 and viscosity 0.1 Pa-s. What is the terminal velocity of the ball?

A) 0.01 m/s B) 0.02 m/s C) 0.04 m/s D) 0.08 m/s

Answer: C) 0.04 m/s

**Explanation: According to Stoke’s law, the drag force F acting upward on a spherical object with very small Reynolds number in a viscous fluid is given by:

F = 6 π r η v

where r is the radius of the sphere, η is the viscosity of the fluid, and v is the velocity of the sphere.

When the sphere reaches terminal velocity, the drag force is balanced by the net gravitational force acting downward on the sphere, which is given by:

F = (ρ_s - ρ_f) g V

where ρ_s and ρ_f are the densities of the sphere and the fluid respectively, g is the acceleration due to gravity, and V is the volume of the sphere.

Equating these two expressions for F, we get:

6 π r η v = (ρ_s - ρ_f) g V

Solving for v, we get:

v = (ρ_s - ρ_f) g V / (6 π r η)

Substituting the given values, we get:

v = (800 - 1000) x 9.8 x (4/3) π x (0.001)^3 / (6 π x 0.001 x 0.1)

v = -0.04 m/s

The negative sign indicates that the sphere is falling downward. The magnitude of the terminal velocity is 0.04 m/s.

 turbulent flow.

Q: Which of the following factors can cause turbulence in a fluid flow?

A) High velocity B) Low viscosity C) Large diameter D) All of the above

Answer: D) All of the above

**Explanation: Turbulence is caused by excessive kinetic energy in parts of a fluid flow, which overcomes the damping effect of the fluid’s viscosity1. When the inertial forces dominate over the viscous forces (when the fluid is flowing faster and Re is larger) then the flow is turbulent2. The Reynolds number is a dimensionless quantity that measures the ratio of inertial to viscous forces and is given by Re = UL/ν, where U is the characteristic velocity, L is the characteristic length, and ν is the kinematic viscosity of the fluid3. If the Reynolds number is greater than Re > 3500, the flow is turbulent2. Therefore, increasing the velocity, decreasing the viscosity, or increasing the diameter can all increase the Reynolds number and cause turbulence.

Bernoulli’s principle and its applications.

Q: A horizontal pipe of cross-sectional area 0.02 m² carries water at a speed of 4 m/s. The pipe narrows to a cross-sectional area of 0.01 m² at a certain point. What is the speed of water at that point?

A) 2 m/s B) 4 m/s C) 8 m/s D) 16 m/s

Answer: C) 8 m/s

**Explanation: Bernoulli’s principle states that an increase in the speed of a fluid occurs simultaneously with a decrease in static pressure or the fluid’s potential energy1. It can be derived from the principle of conservation of energy, which states that, in a steady flow, the sum of all forms of energy in a fluid is the same at all points that are free of viscous forces1. Bernoulli’s equation in its usual form is given by2:

P+21​ρv2+ρgh=constant

Where,

P is the static pressure

ρ is the density of the fluid

v is the speed of the fluid

g is the acceleration due to gravity

h is the height of the fluid

For a horizontal pipe, the height term can be ignored, and the equation can be simplified to:

P+21​ρv2=constant

Since the pipe is horizontal, the pressure at both ends must be equal. Therefore, we can write:

21​ρv12​=21​ρv22​

Where,

v1​ is the speed of water at the wider end

v2​ is the speed of water at the narrower end

Solving for v2​, we get:

v2​=v1​ Sqrt (A1/A2)

Where,

A1​ is the cross-sectional area at the wider end

A2​ is the cross-sectional area at the narrower end

Substituting the given values, we get:

v2​=4Sqrt(0.02/0.01)

v2​=8 m/s

application of surface tension – drops, bubbles and capillary rise.

Here is a possible non-analytical simpler question based on application of surface tension:

Q: Which of the following is an example of surface tension in daily life?

A) Water droplets on a lotus leaf B) Water rising in a capillary tube C) Soap bubbles floating in the air D) All of the above

Answer: D) All of the above

**Explanation: Surface tension is the tendency of a liquid to minimize its surface area by forming a thin film or skin on its surface. Surface tension is caused by the cohesive forces between the molecules of the liquid1.

Water droplets on a lotus leaf are an example of surface tension because they form spherical shapes due to the water molecules pulling each other inward. The water droplets do not wet the leaf because the leaf has a waxy coating that repels water2.

Water rising in a capillary tube is an example of surface tension because it is caused by the adhesive forces between the water molecules and the walls of the tube. The water molecules stick to the walls and pull each other upward, overcoming gravity3.

Soap bubbles floating in the air are an example of surface tension because they are formed by a thin layer of water sandwiched between two layers of soap molecules. The water layer contracts due to surface tension, creating a spherical shape. The soap molecules reduce the surface tension of water, making it easier to form bubbles42.

 

Heat,

Here is a possible non-analytical simpler question based on heat:

Q: What is the unit of heat in the SI system?

A) Calorie B) Joule C) Watt D) Kelvin

Answer: B) Joule

**Explanation: Heat is a form of energy that flows from a higher temperature body to a lower temperature body. The amount of heat transferred can be measured by the amount of work done by the heat. The SI unit of energy and work is joule (J), which is defined as the work done by a force of one newton when its point of application moves one meter in the direction of the force123.

Calorie is another unit of heat, which is defined as the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one gram of water by one degree Celsius. One calorie is equal to 4.184 joules14.

Watt is a unit of power, which is the rate of doing work or transferring energy. One watt is equal to one joule per second4.

Kelvin is a unit of temperature, which measures the average kinetic energy of the molecules in a substance. It is not a unit of heat or energy4.

 

temperature,

Here is a possible non-analytical moderately tough question based on temperature:

Q: Which of the following statements is true about temperature?

A) Temperature is the same as heat B) Temperature is the measure of the total kinetic energy of the particles in an object C) Temperature is the measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in an object D) Temperature is the measure of the potential energy of the particles in an object

Answer: C) Temperature is the measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in an object

**Explanation: Temperature is a physical quantity that expresses how hot or cold an object is. It reflects the kinetic energy of the vibrating and colliding atoms or molecules that make up a substance1. The higher the temperature, the faster the particles move and vice versa.

Heat is a form of energy that flows from a higher temperature body to a lower temperature body. Heat is not the same as temperature, but it depends on it23.

The total kinetic energy of the particles in an object is proportional to its mass and temperature. However, different objects with the same mass and temperature may have different total kinetic energies, depending on their specific heat capacities. Therefore, temperature is not the measure of the total kinetic energy, but the average kinetic energy per particle45.

The potential energy of the particles in an object depends on their positions and interactions with each other. It does not depend on their motion or temperature. Therefore, temperature is not the measure of the potential energy3.

**Explanation: Statement C is false about heat conduction because it describes another form of thermal energy transfer called convection.
