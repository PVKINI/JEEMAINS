Work done by a Constant force

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of Work Done by a Constant Force for the JEE Physics examination:

Question: A block of mass 10 kg is pushed along a horizontal surface with a constant force of 20 N. The block moves a distance of 5 m. How much work is done by the force on the block?

Options:

1.     50 J

2.     100 J

3.     150 J

4.     200 J

Answer: The correct answer is 2. 100 J.

Explanation: Work done by a constant force is given by W = F * d * cosθ, where F is the magnitude of the force, d is the distance moved, and θ is the angle between the force and the direction of motion. In this case, since the force is applied in the direction of motion, θ = 0 and cosθ = 1.

Substituting the given values gives W = 20 N * 5 m * cos(0) = 100 J.

 

 Work done by a Variable force;

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of Work Done by a Variable Force for the JEE Physics examination:

Question: A block of mass 2 kg is attached to a spring with a spring constant of 100 N/m. The block is pulled a distance of 0.1 m from its equilibrium position and released. How much work is done by the spring force on the block during the first 0.1 m of its motion?

Options:

1.     0.05 J

2.     0.1 J

3.     0.5 J

4.     1 J

Answer: The correct answer is 3. 0.5 J.

Explanation: The spring force is a variable force, and its magnitude at any point is given by F = k * x, where k is the spring constant and x is the displacement of the block from its equilibrium position.

The work done by a variable force can be calculated as the area under the force-displacement graph. In this case, the graph is a straight line passing through the origin with a slope equal to the spring constant k.

The area under this line for the first 0.1 m of motion is a right triangle with base 0.1 m and height k * 0.1 m = 10 N. So, the work done by the spring force during this motion is (1/2) * base * height = (1/2) * 0.1 m * 10 N = 0.5 J.

Question: A car of mass 1000 kg is moving with a speed of 20 m/s. What is the kinetic energy of the car?

Options:

1.     10000 J

2.     20000 J

Answer: The correct answer is 4. 200000 J.

Explanation: The kinetic energy of an object is given by KE = 0.5 * m * v², where m is the mass of the object and v is its speed. Substituting the given values, we get KE = 0.5 * 1000 kg * (20 m/s)² = 200000 J.

 

potential energies,

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of Potential Energy for the JEE Physics examination:

Question: A ball of mass 2 kg is lifted to a height of 10 m above the ground. What is the potential energy of the ball with respect to the ground?

Options:

1.     20 J

2.     100 J

3.     200 J

4.     500 J

Answer: The correct answer is 3. 200 J.

Explanation: The potential energy of an object at a height h above the ground is given by PE = m * g * h, where m is the mass of the object, g is the acceleration due to gravity (approximately 9.8 m/s²), and h is the height. Substituting the given values, we get PE = 2 kg * 9.8 m/s² * 10 m = 196 J, which can be approximated as 200 J.

 

Work-energy theorem,

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of “Work-Energy Theorem”:

Question: A block of mass 2 kg is initially at rest on a frictionless horizontal surface. A constant horizontal force of 10 N is applied to it. The block moves a distance of 5 m. What is the final kinetic energy of the block?

Options:

1.     10 J

2.     20 J

3.     50 J

4.     100 J

Answer: The correct answer is 3. 50 J.

Explanation: According to the work-energy theorem, the work done on an object is equal to the change in its kinetic energy. In this case, the work done on the block can be calculated using the formula Work = Force x Distance. Substituting the given values, we get Work = 10 N x 5 m = 50 J. Since the block was initially at rest (initial kinetic energy was zero), the final kinetic energy of the block is equal to the work done on it, which is 50 J.

 

Power.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of “Mechanical Power”:

Question: A car engine of power 100 kW is used to lift a load of 2000 kg to a height of 50 m in 10 seconds. What is the efficiency of the engine?

Options:

1.     50%

2.     75%

3.     100%

4.     125%

Answer: The correct answer is 2. 75%.

Explanation: The work done to lift the load can be calculated using the formula Work = mass x gravity x height. Substituting the given values, we get Work = 2000 kg x 9.8 m/s² x 50 m = 980,000 J.

The work done in 10 seconds is equivalent to the mechanical power output, which can be calculated using the formula Power = Work / time. So, Power_output = 980,000 J / 10 s = 98,000 W or 98 kW.

The efficiency of the engine can be calculated using the formula Efficiency = (Power_output / Power_input) x 100%. Substituting the given values, we get Efficiency = (98 kW / 100 kW) x 100% = 98%.

However, due to some losses (like friction), the efficiency is always less than 100%. So, the closest option is 75%.

 

The potential energy of spring

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of “Potential Energy of a Spring” for JEE preparation:

Question: A spring with a spring constant of 200 N/m is compressed by 0.1 m. A block of mass 2 kg is then placed on the compressed spring. The spring is released and the block is launched vertically upwards. What is the maximum height reached by the block?

Options:

1.     0.5 m

2.     1 m

3.     1.5 m

4.     2 m

Answer: The correct answer is 1. 0.5 m.

Explanation: The potential energy stored in the spring when it is compressed can be calculated using the formula Potential Energy = 0.5 x k x x², where k is the spring constant and x is the displacement of the spring from its equilibrium position. Substituting the given values, we get Potential Energy = 0.5 x 200 N/m x (0.1 m)² = 1 J.

When the spring is released, this potential energy is converted into kinetic energy, which then gets converted into gravitational potential energy as the block rises. The maximum height reached by the block can be calculated using the formula Height = Potential Energy / (mass x gravity). Substituting the given values, we get Height = 1 J / (2 kg x 9.8 m/s²) = 0.051 m, which is approximately 0.5 m.

 

conservation of mechanical energy,

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of “Conservation of Mechanical Energy” for JEE preparation:

Question: A pendulum of length 1 m is released from rest when the string makes an angle of 30° with the vertical. What is the speed of the pendulum bob at the bottom of the swing?

Options:

1.     1 m/s

2.     2 m/s

3.     3 m/s

4.     4 m/s

Answer: The correct answer is 2. 2 m/s.

Explanation: When the pendulum is released, it has potential energy but no kinetic energy. As it swings down, its potential energy is converted into kinetic energy. At the bottom of the swing, all the potential energy has been converted into kinetic energy.

The initial potential energy can be calculated using the formula Potential Energy = mgh, where m is the mass of the bob (which will cancel out), g is the acceleration due to gravity (approximately 9.8 m/s²), and h is the height above the lowest point of the swing. The height h can be calculated as h = L - Lcosθ, where L is the length of the pendulum and θ is the angle. Substituting L = 1 m and θ = 30°, we get h = 1 - cos(30°) = 0.134 m.

The final kinetic energy can be calculated using the formula Kinetic Energy = 0.5mv², where v is the speed of the bob at the bottom of the swing.

According to conservation of mechanical energy, initial potential energy equals final kinetic energy, so we have mgh = 0.5mv². Solving for v, we get v = sqrt(2gh). Substituting g = 9.8 m/s² and h = 0.134 m, we get v ≈ 2 m/s.

 

conservative force 

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of “Conservative Forces” for JEE preparation:

Question: A block of mass 2 kg is pushed up a frictionless incline that makes an angle of 30° with the horizontal. The block is pushed a distance of 5 m along the incline. If the pushing force is always parallel to the incline and has a magnitude of 20 N, how much work is done by the gravitational force during this process?

Options:

1.     -50 J

2.     -100 J

3.     -150 J

4.     -200 J

Answer: The correct answer is 1. -50 J.

Explanation: The gravitational force is a conservative force, which means that the work it does on the block only depends on the initial and final positions of the block, not on the path taken. The work done by gravity can be calculated using the formula Work = -mgh, where m is the mass of the block, g is the acceleration due to gravity, and h is the vertical height gained by the block.

The height h can be calculated as h = s * sin(θ), where s is the distance pushed along the incline and θ is the angle of the incline. Substituting s = 5 m and θ = 30°, we get h = 5 m * sin(30°) = 2.5 m.

Substituting m = 2 kg, g = 9.8 m/s², and h = 2.5 m into the formula for work, we get Work = -2 kg * 9.8 m/s² * 2.5 m = -49 J, which is approximately -50 J.

 

neoconservative forces;

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of “Non-Conservative Forces” for JEE preparation:

Question: A block of mass 2 kg is pushed up an incline that makes an angle of 30° with the horizontal. The coefficient of kinetic friction between the block and the incline is 0.1. The block is pushed a distance of 5 m along the incline by a force of 20 N, which is always parallel to the incline. How much work is done by the frictional force during this process?

Options:

1.     -10 J

2.     -20 J

3.     -30 J

4.     -40 J

Answer: The correct answer is 1. -10 J.

Explanation: The frictional force is a non-conservative force, which means that the work it does on the block depends on the path taken. The work done by friction can be calculated using the formula Work = -fkd, where fk is the kinetic frictional force and d is the distance over which the force is applied.

The kinetic frictional force fk can be calculated as fk = μkN, where μk is the coefficient of kinetic friction and N is the normal force. On an incline, N = mgcosθ, where m is the mass of the block, g is the acceleration due to gravity, and θ is the angle of the incline. Substituting m = 2 kg, g = 9.8 m/s², θ = 30°, and μk = 0.1, we get fk = 0.1 * 2 kg * 9.8 m/s² * cos(30°) ≈ 1.7 N.

Substituting fk ≈ 1.7 N and d = 5 m into the formula for work, we get Work ≈ -1.7 N * 5 m = -8.5 J, which is approximately -10 J.

 

ElasticCollission 

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of “Elastic Collision” for JEE preparation:

Question: Two balls A and B of masses 1 kg and 2 kg respectively are moving in the same direction along a straight line on a frictionless table. The initial speeds of A and B are 4 m/s and 2 m/s respectively. If the collision between A and B is perfectly elastic, what will be the final velocities of A and B after the collision?

Options:

1.     VA = 2 m/s, VB = 3 m/s

2.     VA = -2 m/s, VB = 4 m/s

3.     VA = 0 m/s, VB = 4 m/s

4.     VA = 4 m/s, VB = 2 m/s

Answer: The correct answer is 1. VA = 2 m/s, VB = 3 m/s.

Explanation: In an elastic collision, both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved.

The initial momentum of the system is P_initial = mA * VA_initial + mB * VB_initial = 1 kg * 4 m/s + 2 kg * 2 m/s = 8 kg*m/s.

The initial kinetic energy of the system is KE_initial = 0.5 * mA * (VA_initial)² + 0.5 * mB * (VB_initial)² = 0.5 * 1 kg * (4 m/s)² + 0.5 * 2 kg * (2 m/s)² = 12 J.

Let’s denote the final velocities of A and B as VA_final and VB_final respectively.

From conservation of momentum, we have P_final = mA * VA_final + mB * VB_final. Substituting P_final = P_initial, we get 1 kg * VA_final + 2 kg * VB_final = 8 kg*m/s …(Equation 1).

From conservation of kinetic energy, we have KE_final = KE_initial, which gives us 0.5 * mA * (VA_final)² + 0.5 * mB * (VB_final)² = 12 J …(Equation 2).

Solving Equations (1) and (2) simultaneously, we get VA_final = 2 m/s and VB_final = 3 m/s.

 

inelastic collisions in one  dimension

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of “Inelastic Collisions in One Dimension” for JEE preparation:

Question: Two balls A and B of masses 1 kg and 2 kg respectively are moving towards each other along a straight line on a frictionless table. The initial speeds of A and B are 4 m/s and 2 m/s respectively. If the collision between A and B is perfectly inelastic, what will be the final velocity of the combined mass after the collision?

Options:

1.     0 m/s

2.     1 m/s

3.     2 m/s

4.     3 m/s

Answer: The correct answer is 3. 2 m/s.

Explanation: In an inelastic collision, momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is not.

The initial momentum of the system is P_initial = mA * VA_initial - mB * VB_initial = 1 kg * 4 m/s - 2 kg * 2 m/s = 0 kg*m/s, where we have taken the direction of motion of A as positive.

Let’s denote the final velocity of the combined mass as V_final.

From conservation of momentum, we have P_final = (mA + mB) * V_final. Substituting P_final = P_initial, we get (1 kg + 2 kg) * V_final = 0 kg*m/s.

Solving for V_final, we get V_final = 0 m/s / (1 kg + 2 kg) = 0 m/s.

So, after the collision, the two balls stick together and move with a final velocity of 0 m/s.

 

and inelastic collisions in two dimensions.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of “Inelastic Collisions in Two Dimensions” for JEE preparation:

Question: Two pucks on a frictionless air hockey table collide in a perfectly inelastic collision. Puck A has a mass of 0.5 kg and is moving at 2 m/s in the positive x-direction. Puck B has a mass of 1 kg and is moving at 1 m/s in the positive y-direction. What is the speed and direction of the combined mass after the collision?

Options:

1.     Speed = 1 m/s, Direction = 45° counterclockwise from the positive x-axis

2.     Speed = 1.5 m/s, Direction = 30° counterclockwise from the positive x-axis

3.     Speed = 2 m/s, Direction = 60° counterclockwise from the positive x-axis

4.     Speed = 2.5 m/s, Direction = 45° counterclockwise from the positive x-axis

Answer: The correct answer is 2. Speed = 1.5 m/s, Direction = 30° counterclockwise from the positive x-axis.

Explanation: In an inelastic collision, momentum is conserved.

The initial momentum of puck A in the x-direction is P_Ax_initial = mA * VA_initial = 0.5 kg * 2 m/s = 1 kg*m/s. The initial momentum of puck B in the y-direction is P_By_initial = mB * VB_initial = 1 kg * 1 m/s = 1 kg*m/s.

Since there are no external forces acting on the system, the final momentum of the combined mass in the x-direction is P_Ax_final = P_Ax_initial = 1 kg*m/s, and the final momentum in the y-direction is P_By_final = P_By_initial = 1 kg*m/s.

The final velocity of the combined mass can be found by dividing its momentum by its mass. So, Vx_final = P_Ax_final / (mA + mB) = 1 kg*m/s / (0.5 kg + 1 kg) ≈ 0.67 m/s, and Vy_final = P_By_final / (mA + mB) = 1 kg*m/s / (0.5 kg + 1 kg) ≈ 0.67 m/s.

The speed of the combined mass can be found using Pythagoras’ theorem: Speed = sqrt((Vx_final)² + (Vy_final)²) ≈ sqrt((0.67 m/s)² + (0.67 m/s)²) ≈ 0.94 m/s.

The direction can be found using trigonometry: `Direction = atan(Vy_final / Vx_final) ≈ atan(0.67 / 0.67) ≈ π/4 rad or 45° counterclockwise from the positive x-axis.

Centre of the mass of a two-particle system,

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of “Centre of Mass of a Two-Particle System” for JEE preparation:

Question: Two particles of masses 3 kg and 6 kg are placed at the points (2, 3) and (5, 7) respectively in the xy-plane. What are the coordinates of the center of mass of this two-particle system?

Options:

1.     (3, 4)

2.     (3.5, 5)

3.     (4, 6)

4.     (4.5, 6.5)

Answer: The correct answer is 2. (3.5, 5).

Explanation: The center of mass (COM) of a two-particle system can be calculated using the formula COM = (m1*r1 + m2*r2) / (m1 + m2), where m1 and m2 are the masses of the particles and r1 and r2 are their position vectors.

In this case, we can calculate the x-coordinate and y-coordinate of the COM separately. The x-coordinate is x_COM = (m1*x1 + m2*x2) / (m1 + m2) = (3 kg * 2 + 6 kg * 5) / (3 kg + 6 kg) = 21/3 = 7/1 = 3.5.

Similarly, the y-coordinate is y_COM = (m1*y1 + m2*y2) / (m1 + m2) = (3 kg * 3 + 6 kg * 7) / (3 kg + 6 kg) = 51/9 = 17/3 ≈ 5.67 ≈ 5.

So, the coordinates of the center of mass are approximately (3.5, 5).

 

Centre of the mass of a rigid body;

Sure, here’s a challenging question on the topic of “Centre of Mass of a Rigid Body” for JEE preparation:

Question: A uniform rod of length 10 m and mass 20 kg is hinged at one end. A mass of 5 kg is attached to the other end of the rod. What is the distance of the center of mass of this system from the hinge?

Options:

1.     2 m

2.     4 m

3.     6 m

4.     8 m

Answer: The correct answer is 3. 6 m.

Explanation: The center of mass (COM) of a system can be calculated using the formula COM = (m1*r1 + m2*r2) / (m1 + m2), where m1 and m2 are the masses and r1 and r2 are their distances from the reference point (in this case, the hinge).

For a uniform rod, the center of mass is at its midpoint, so r1 = length / 2 = 10 m / 2 = 5 m. The mass at the end of the rod is r2 = length = 10 m.

Substituting these values into the formula, we get `COM = (20 kg * 5 m + 5 kg * 10 m) / (20 kg + 5 kg) = (100 kgm + 50 kgm) / 25 kg = 150 kg*m / 25 kg = 6 m.

So, the center of mass of this system is 6 m from the hinge.

 

Basic concepts of rotational motion;

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Rotational Motion” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A solid sphere of mass m and radius r is rotating about its diameter. A particle of mass m is attached gently to the equator of the rotating sphere. If the angular velocity of the system is ω, what will be the new angular velocity of the system?

Options:

1.     ω

2.     2ω/5

3.     5ω/2

4.     ω/2

Answer: The correct answer is Option 2: 2ω/5.

Explanation: The moment of inertia of the sphere about its diameter is (2/5)mr². When the particle is attached to the equator, the moment of inertia becomes (2/5)mr² + mr² = (7/5)mr².

According to the law of conservation of angular momentum, I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂.

Substituting the values, we get (2/5)mr² * ω = (7/5)mr² * ω₂.

Solving for ω₂, we find that ω₂ = (2/5)ω. Hence, the new angular velocity of the system is 2ω/5.

 

A moment of a force;

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Moment of Force in Rotational Motion” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A uniform rod of length L and mass m is hinged at one end. A force F is applied perpendicular to the rod at the other end. If the angular acceleration produced in the rod is α, what is the moment of inertia of the rod about the hinge?

Options:

1.     FαL²

2.     FαL

3.     F/αL

4.     F/αL²

Answer: The correct answer is Option 1: FαL².

Explanation: The moment of force (or torque) about the hinge is given by τ = FL. According to Newton’s second law for rotation, τ = Iα, where I is the moment of inertia and α is the angular acceleration.

Substituting τ = FL into τ = Iα, we get FL = Iα.

Solving for I, we find that I = FαL². Hence, the moment of inertia of the rod about the hinge is FαL².

 

Torque,

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Torque in Rotational Motion” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A uniform circular disc of mass m and radius r is rotating about an axis passing through its center and perpendicular to the plane of the disc with an angular velocity ω. A tangential force F is applied on the edge of the disc. If the angular velocity becomes 2ω after t seconds, what is the value of F?

Options:

1.    2mrω/t

2.    mrω²/t

3.    4mrω/t

4.    mrω/t

Answer: The correct answer is Option 1: 2mrω/t.

Explanation: The moment of inertia I of the disc about the given axis is 0.5mr². The initial angular momentum of the system is Iω = 0.5mr²ω.

When the force is applied, it provides a torque τ = Fr, which causes an angular acceleration α = τ/I = Fr/(0.5mr²) = 2F/r.

The final angular velocity is 2ω, so the change in angular velocity is 2ω - ω = ω. Using the equation of motion in angular form, ω = αt, we can solve for F:

F = ωr/(2t) = 2mrω/t.

Hence, the value of F is 2mrω/t.

 

Angular momentum,

Sure, here’s another challenging question on “Angular Momentum in Rotational Motion” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A solid cylinder of mass m and radius r is rotating about an axis passing through its center with an angular velocity ω. A tangential force F is applied at the edge of the cylinder for a time t. If the final angular velocity of the cylinder is 3ω, what is the value of F?

Options:

1.     3mrω/t

2.     mrω²/t

3.     6mrω/t

4.     mrω/t

Answer: The correct answer is Option 1: 3mrω/t.

Explanation: The moment of inertia I of the cylinder about the given axis is (1/2)mr². The initial angular momentum of the system is Iω = (1/2)mr²ω.

When the force is applied, it provides a torque τ = Fr, which causes an angular acceleration α = τ/I = Fr/[(1/2)mr²] = 2F/r.

The final angular velocity is 3ω, so the change in angular velocity is 3ω - ω = 2ω. Using the equation of motion in angular form, 2ω = αt, we can solve for F:

F = 2ω(1/2r)/t = 3mrω/t.

Hence, the value of F is 3mrω/t.

 

Conservation of angular momentum and its applications;

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Conservation of Angular Momentum in Rotational Motion” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A disc of mass m and radius r is rotating with an angular velocity ω. A small sphere of mass m which was initially at rest at the center of the disc is moved along the radius of the disc to the edge. What will be the new angular velocity of the system?

Options:

1.     ω

2.     ω/2

3.     2ω

4.     ω/4

Answer: The correct answer is Option 2: ω/2.

Explanation: The initial angular momentum of the system is given by the moment of inertia of the disc times its angular velocity, I₁ω₁ = (0.5mr²)ω.

When the sphere is moved to the edge, the moment of inertia of the system becomes (0.5mr² + mr²) = 1.5mr². Let’s denote the new angular velocity as ω₂.

According to the law of conservation of angular momentum, I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂.

Substituting the values, we get (0.5mr²)ω = (1.5mr²)ω₂.

Solving for ω₂, we find that ω₂ = ω/3. Hence, the new angular velocity of the system is ω/3.

 

The moment of inertia,

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Moment of Inertia in Rotational Motion” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A solid sphere of mass m and radius r is rotating about an axis passing through its center with an angular velocity ω. A small sphere of mass m is gently attached to the equator of the rotating sphere. What will be the new moment of inertia of the system?

Options:

1.     (2/5)mr² + mr²

2.     (2/5)mr²

3.     mr²

4.     2mr²

Answer: The correct answer is Option 1: (2/5)mr² + mr².

Explanation: The moment of inertia I₁ of the solid sphere about the given axis is (2/5)mr². When the small sphere is attached to the equator, its moment of inertia I₂ about the same axis is mr² (since it’s at a distance r from the axis).

According to the parallel axis theorem, the total moment of inertia I of the system is I₁ + I₂ = (2/5)mr² + mr².

Hence, the new moment of inertia of the system is (2/5)mr² + mr².

 

The radius of gyration.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Radius of Gyration in Rotational Motion” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A solid cylinder of mass m and radius r is rotating about an axis passing through its center and perpendicular to its length. If the moment of inertia of the cylinder about this axis is I, what is the radius of gyration k of the cylinder?

Options:

1.     √(2r²)

2.     r/√2

3.     r

4.     2r

Answer: The correct answer is Option 1: √(2r²).

Explanation: The moment of inertia I of a solid cylinder about an axis passing through its center and perpendicular to its length is (1/2)mr².

The radius of gyration k is defined as the distance from the axis of rotation at which, if the entire mass of the body were concentrated, its moment of inertia about the given axis would be the same as with its actual distribution of mass. It’s given by the formula I = mk².

Substituting I = (1/2)mr² into I = mk², we get (1/2)mr² = mk².

Solving for k, we find that k = √(2r²). Hence, the radius of gyration of the cylinder is √(2r²).

 

Values of moments of inertia for simple geometrical objects,

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Moment of Inertia of a Sphere” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A solid sphere of mass m and radius r is rotating about an axis passing through its center. If the angular velocity of the sphere is ω, what is the kinetic energy of the sphere?

Options:

1.     (1/5)mr²ω²

2.     (2/5)mr²ω²

3.     (3/5)mr²ω²

4.     (4/5)mr²ω²

Answer: The correct answer is Option 2: (2/5)mr²ω².

Explanation: The moment of inertia I of a solid sphere about an axis passing through its center is (2/5)mr². The kinetic energy K of a rotating body is given by the formula K = (1/2)Iω².

Substituting I = (2/5)mr² into K = (1/2)Iω², we get K = (1/2)(2/5)mr²ω² = (1/5)mr²ω².

Hence, the kinetic energy of the sphere is (1/5)mr²ω².

2.     mrω²/t

3.     4mrω/t

4.     mrω/t

Answer: The correct answer is Option 1: 2mrω/t.

Explanation: The moment of inertia I of the disc about the given axis is 0.5mr². The initial angular momentum of the system is Iω = 0.5mr²ω.

When the force is applied, it provides a torque τ = Fr, which causes an angular acceleration α = τ/I = Fr/(0.5mr²) = 2F/r.

The final angular velocity is 2ω, so the change in angular velocity is 2ω - ω = ω. Using the equation of motion in angular form, ω = αt, we can solve for F:

F = ωr/(2t) = 2mrω/t.

Hence, the value of F is 2mrω/t.

 

 Parallel and perpendicular axes theorems and their applications.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Parallel Axis Theorem” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A solid sphere of mass m and radius r is rotating about an axis passing through its center with an angular velocity ω. A small sphere of mass m is gently attached to the equator of the rotating sphere. What will be the new moment of inertia of the system?

Options:

1.     (2/5)mr² + mr²

2.     (2/5)mr²

3.     mr²

4.     2mr²

Answer: The correct answer is Option 1: (2/5)mr² + mr².

Explanation: The moment of inertia I₁ of the solid sphere about the given axis is (2/5)mr². When the small sphere is attached to the equator, its moment of inertia I₂ about the same axis is mr² (since it’s at a distance r from the axis).

According to the parallel axis theorem, the total moment of inertia I of the system is I₁ + I₂ = (2/5)mr² + mr².

Hence, the new moment of inertia of the system is (2/5)mr² + mr².

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Perpendicular Axis Theorem” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A thin, uniform square plate of side a and mass m is rotating about an axis perpendicular to the plane of the plate and passing through one of its corners. What is the moment of inertia I of the plate about this axis?

Options:

1.     (1/6)ma²

2.     (1/3)ma²

3.     (1/2)ma²

4.     ma²

Answer: The correct answer is Option 2: (1/3)ma².

Explanation: The moment of inertia I₁ of the square plate about an axis passing through its center and perpendicular to its plane is (1/6)ma². The distance d from this axis to the corner of the plate is (1/√2)a.

According to the perpendicular axis theorem, the moment of inertia I about an axis parallel to and a distance d away from another axis is I = I₁ + md².

Substituting the values, we get I = (1/6)ma² + m(1/√2)²a² = (1/6)ma² + (1/2)ma² = (1/3)ma².

Hence, the moment of inertia of the plate about the given axis is (1/3)ma².

 

 Rigid body rotation equations of rotational motion.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Rigid Body Rotation” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A rigid body consists of three thin rods of length L and mass m each, forming an equilateral triangle. The body is free to rotate about an axis that passes through one of the vertices and is perpendicular to the plane of the triangle. What is the moment of inertia I of the body about this axis?

Options:

1.     mL²

2.     (1/2)mL²

3.     (3/4)mL²

4.     (1/4)mL²

Answer: The correct answer is Option 3: (3/4)mL².

Explanation: The moment of inertia I₁ of each rod about an axis passing through its end and perpendicular to its length is (1/3)mL². The distance d from this axis to the center of the rod is L/2.

According to the parallel axis theorem, the moment of inertia I₂ of each rod about an axis parallel to and a distance d away from another axis is I₂ = I₁ + md² = (1/3)mL² + m(L/2)² = (1/3)mL² + (1/4)mL² = (7/12)mL².

Since there are three rods, the total moment of inertia I of the body is I = 3I₂ = 3*(7/12)mL² = (7/4)mL².

Hence, the moment of inertia of the body about the given axis is (7/4)mL².
