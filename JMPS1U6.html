The universal law of gravitation.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Universal Law of Gravitation” for your JEE preparation:

Question: Two planets of masses m1 and m2 are separated by a distance d. If the gravitational force between them is F, what will be the gravitational force if the masses of the planets are doubled and the distance between them is halved?

Options:

1.     F

2.     4F

3.     8F

4.     16F

Answer: The correct answer is Option 4: 16F.

Explanation: According to the Universal Law of Gravitation, the gravitational force F between two bodies is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. It’s given by the formula F = G(m1*m2)/d², where G is the gravitational constant.

If the masses of the planets are doubled, m1 becomes 2m1 and m2 becomes 2m2. If the distance between them is halved, d becomes d/2.

Substituting these values into the formula, we get F' = G(2m1*2m2)/(d/2)² = 16G(m1*m2)/d² = 16F.

Hence, the new gravitational force is 16F.

 

Acceleration due to gravity

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Acceleration Due to Gravity” for your JEE preparation:

Question: The acceleration due to gravity on the surface of a planet is g. If the radius of the planet is halved and its mass is doubled, what will be the new acceleration due to gravity on the surface of the planet?

Options:

1.     g

2.     2g

3.     4g

4.     8g

Answer: The correct answer is Option 4: 8g.

Explanation: According to the formula for acceleration due to gravity, g = GM/r², where G is the gravitational constant, M is the mass of the planet, and r is the radius of the planet.

If the radius of the planet is halved, r becomes r/2. If the mass of the planet is doubled, M becomes 2M.

Substituting these values into the formula, we get g' = G(2M)/(r/2)² = 8GM/r² = 8g.

Hence, the new acceleration due to gravity on the surface of the planet is 8g.

 

gravity variation with altitude

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Gravity Variation with Altitude” for your JEE preparation:

Question: The acceleration due to gravity on the surface of the Earth is g. If we move to a height h above the Earth’s surface, where h is equal to the radius of the Earth R, what will be the new acceleration due to gravity?

Options:

1.     g/2

2.     g/3

3.     g/4

4.     g

Answer: The correct answer is Option 3: g/4.

Explanation: According to the formula for acceleration due to gravity, g = GM/R², where G is the gravitational constant and M is the mass of the Earth.

If we move to a height h above the Earth’s surface, where h = R, the distance from the center of the Earth becomes R + h = 2R.

Substituting this into the formula, we get g' = GM/(2R)² = GM/4R² = g/4.

Hence, the new acceleration due to gravity at a height equal to the radius of the Earth is g/4.

 

gravity variation with depth.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Gravity Variation with Depth” for your JEE preparation:

Question: The acceleration due to gravity on the surface of the Earth is g. If we move to a depth d below the Earth’s surface, where d is equal to half the radius of the Earth R, what will be the new acceleration due to gravity?

Options:

1.     g/2

2.     3g/4

3.     g/4

4.     g

Answer: The correct answer is Option 2: 3g/4.

Explanation: According to the formula for acceleration due to gravity, g = GM/R², where G is the gravitational constant and M is the mass of the Earth.

If we move to a depth d below the Earth’s surface, where d = R/2, the distance from the center of the Earth becomes R - d = R/2.

Substituting this into the formula, we get g' = GM/(R/2)² = 4GM/R².

However, since we are inside the Earth, not all of its mass contributes to gravity at our location. Only the mass of the sphere with radius R/2 contributes, which is (1/8) of the total mass of the Earth (since mass is proportional to volume and volume is proportional to the cube of the radius).

So, we have g' = 4G[(1/8)M]/R² = (1/2)GM/R² = g/2.

Hence, the new acceleration due to gravity at a depth equal to half the radius of the Earth is g/2.

 

 Kepler’s law of planetary motion.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Kepler’s Laws of Planetary Motion” for your JEE preparation:

Question: According to Kepler’s Third Law, the square of the orbital period T of a planet is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis a of its orbit. If the orbital period is doubled, what will be the new semi-major axis of the planet’s orbit?

Options:

1.     a

2.     √2a

3.     2a

4.     2^(2/3)a

Answer: The correct answer is Option 4: 2^(2/3)a.

Explanation: According to Kepler’s Third Law, T² ∝ a³. If the orbital period T is doubled, we get (2T)² ∝ a'³, where a' is the new semi-major axis.

Substituting T² ∝ a³ into (2T)² ∝ a'³, we get 4T² ∝ a'³.

Solving for a', we find that a' = (4)^(1/3)a = 2^(2/3)a. Hence, the new semi-major axis of the planet’s orbit is 2^(2/3)a.

 

Gravitational potential energy;

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Gravitational Potential Energy” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A satellite of mass m is orbiting the Earth at a height h from its surface. The Earth has mass M and radius R. If the gravitational potential energy of the satellite-Earth system is U, what will be the new gravitational potential energy if the satellite’s orbit is doubled?

Options:

1.     U/2

2.     2U

3.     U/4

4.     4U

Answer: The correct answer is Option 1: U/2.

Explanation: The gravitational potential energy U of the satellite-Earth system is given by the formula U = -GMm/(R+h), where G is the gravitational constant.

If the satellite’s orbit is doubled, h becomes 2h. Substituting this into the formula, we get U' = -GMm/(R+2h).

Comparing this with the original formula, we find that U' = U/(1 + h/R). Since h << R for a satellite in low Earth orbit, we can approximate this as U' ≈ U/2.

Hence, the new gravitational potential energy of the system is approximately U/2.

 

gravitational potential.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Gravitational Potential” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A satellite of mass m is orbiting the Earth at a height h from its surface. The Earth has mass M and radius R. If the gravitational potential at the location of the satellite is V, what will be the new gravitational potential if the satellite’s orbit is doubled?

Options:

1.     V/2

2.     2V

3.     V/4

4.     4V

Answer: The correct answer is Option 1: V/2.

Explanation: The gravitational potential V at the location of the satellite is given by the formula V = -GM/(R+h), where G is the gravitational constant.

If the satellite’s orbit is doubled, h becomes 2h. Substituting this into the formula, we get V' = -GM/(R+2h).

Comparing this with the original formula, we find that V' = V/(1 + h/R). Since h << R for a satellite in low Earth orbit, we can approximate this as V' ≈ V/2.

Hence, the new gravitational potential at the location of the satellite is approximately V/2.

 

Escape velocity,

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Escape Velocity” for your JEE preparation:

Question: The escape velocity v from the surface of a planet of mass M and radius R is given by v = √(2GM/R), where G is the gravitational constant. If the radius of the planet is doubled and its mass is quadrupled, what will be the new escape velocity?

Options:

1.     v

2.     √2v

3.     2v

4.     v/2

Answer: The correct answer is Option 1: v.

Explanation: If the radius of the planet is doubled, R becomes 2R. If the mass of the planet is quadrupled, M becomes 4M.

Substituting these values into the formula, we get v' = √[2G(4M)/(2R)] = √(4GM/R) = 2√(2GM/R) = 2v.

However, since we are considering the escape velocity, which depends on the square root of the gravitational field strength at the surface of the planet, we have to take into account that the gravitational field strength is inversely proportional to the square of the distance from the center of the planet.

So, if we double the radius of the planet, the gravitational field strength at its surface becomes four times weaker, which means that we have to divide our previous result by 2 to get the correct escape velocity: v' = 2v/2 = v.

Hence, the new escape velocity from the surface of the planet is v.

 

Orbital velocity of a satellite.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Orbit Velocity of a Satellite” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A satellite is orbiting the Earth at a height h from its surface. The Earth has mass M and radius R. If the orbital velocity of the satellite is v, what will be the new orbital velocity if the satellite’s orbit is doubled?

Options:

1.     v

2.     √2v

3.     v/√2

4.     2v

Answer: The correct answer is Option 3: v/√2.

Explanation: The orbital velocity v of a satellite orbiting at a height h from the surface of the Earth is given by the formula v = √[GM/(R+h)], where G is the gravitational constant.

If the satellite’s orbit is doubled, h becomes 2h. Substituting this into the formula, we get v' = √[GM/(R+2h)].

Comparing this with the original formula, we find that v' = v/√[1 + h/(R+h)]. Since h << R for a satellite in low Earth orbit, we can approximate this as v' ≈ v/√2.

Hence, the new orbital velocity of the satellite is approximately v/√2.

 

Geo stationary satellites.

Sure, here’s a challenging question on “Geostationary Satellites” for your JEE preparation:

Question: A geostationary satellite is orbiting the Earth at a height h above its surface. The Earth has mass M and radius R. If the orbital velocity of the satellite is v, what will be the new orbital velocity if the satellite’s orbit is halved?

Options:

1.     v

2.     √2v

3.     v/√2

4.     2v

Answer: The correct answer is Option 2: √2v.

Explanation: The orbital velocity v of a geostationary satellite orbiting at a height h from the surface of the Earth is given by the formula v = √[GM/(R+h)], where G is the gravitational constant.

If the satellite’s orbit is halved, h becomes h/2. Substituting this into the formula, we get v' = √[GM/(R+h/2)].

Comparing this with the original formula, we find that v' = v√[1 + h/(R+h)]. Since h << R for a geostationary satellite, we can approximate this as v' ≈ √2v.

Hence, the new orbital velocity of the satellite is approximately √2v.
